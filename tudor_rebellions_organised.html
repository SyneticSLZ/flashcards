BP1.

Causes of Rebellions

Religious

MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
The largest Tudor rebellion — up to 50,000 rebels across 7 northern counties under the Five Wounds banner, led by Robert Aske. Presented 24 Pontefract Articles against the dissolution of 100+ monasteries and the 1534 Subsidy Act.
MiddleWestern (Prayer Book) Rebellion— 1549
~7,000 Devon and Cornwall rebels besieged Exeter for 6 weeks, calling the new English Prayer Book a "Christmas game." Presented 14 articles; led by tailor Thomas Underhill and the vicar Robert Welsh.
EndIrishFitzmaurice Fitzgerald's Rebellions— 1569 & 1579
Munster rebellion. The 1579 revolt brought papal blessing and 600 Spanish and Italian troops at Smerwick — a rare direct foreign Catholic intervention. English reprisals executed 800 people 1569–72.
Synthesis Religious causes only emerge after the break with Rome (1534) — Henry VII's reign saw none. From 1536 they drive or amplify almost every major rising, and by 1579 the religious dimension reaches Ireland with explicit papal involvement.

Socio-Economic

StartYorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Protest against a £100,000 parliamentary grant for the French war. Made worse by a bad harvest in 1488. Tax commissioner Henry Percy was murdered while collecting the tax.
MiddleKett's Rebellion— 1549
16,000 Norfolk rebels camped on Mousehold Heath for 7 weeks. 17 of 29 demands targeted enclosures and rents (rents up 30% since 1548; wheat prices up 50% in 1548). 60% of Norfolk land held by just 46 gentry families.
EndOxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Bartholomew Steer planned to assemble at Enslow Hill against new enclosures — only 4 men turned up. Backdrop of 4 consecutive bad harvests.
Synthesis Socio-economic grievances are the most consistent cause across the period — tax in 1489, rents in 1549, harvests in 1596 — yet popular appetite collapses dramatically: 20,000+ in 1489 versus only 4 in 1596.

Political

StartSimnel Rebellion— 1487
Lambert Simnel posed as the Earl of Warwick, backed by the Earl of Lincoln (Richard III's named heir) and ~2,000 Burgundian mercenaries funded by Margaret of Burgundy — a foreign-backed dynastic challenge to Henry VII's throne.
MiddleIrishSilken Thomas's Rebellion— 1534–37
Although Thomas invoked Catholicism, the rising was primarily political — his objective was to expel the English administration and become sole ruler of Ireland. He and his 5 uncles raised ~1,000 men in Munster and invaded the Pale; the rebellion lasted 3 years.
EndEssex Revolt— 1601
The Earl of Essex — suspended from the Privy Council and ruined after losing his sweet wine patent — marched ~300 supporters through London. Lasted just 12 hours. The last noble rebellion in English history.
Synthesis Political causes shift from foreign-backed dynastic challenges (Simnel) under Henry VII to factional and personal grievances under late Elizabeth (Essex). Irish risings remain territorially political throughout — from Silken Thomas to Tyrone.
BP2.

Frequency & Nature

Lack of Support

StartPerkin Warbeck's Rebellion— 1491–99
Warbeck's final invasion attracted only 1,500 Cornish miners, artisans and farmers — not one was a noble or gentleman despite 8 years of foreign manoeuvring.
MiddleWyatt's Rebellion— 1554
Co-conspirators in Leicestershire (Suffolk), Devon (Carew) and Herefordshire (Croft) all failed to rise. French support sent 0 troops.
EndOxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Steer planned to assemble hundreds at Enslow Hill — only 4 men turned up. Conspirators arrested before the rising could even start.
Synthesis Lack of support becomes increasingly fatal across the period: 1,500 men was already weak by Henry VII's standards, but by Elizabeth's end even credible plots collapse to single digits before assembly.

Poor Leadership

StartLovel & Stafford Rebellion— 1486
Lovel and the Staffords were unable to get enough support from their retainers; Staffords arrested at Culham Church sanctuary; Lovel fled abroad. No battle fought — poor organisation and intelligence failure.
MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Aske had 40,000 men at Pontefract but "had no wish to do battle" with Norfolk's troops. Henry's 2 false pardons were never overcome.
EndEssex Revolt— 1601
Essex was a good strategist but poor tactician — misjudged London's willingness to support him; the City refused to rise; surrendered in 12 hours.
Synthesis Leadership weaknesses recur across the period regardless of cause — failures of nerve (Aske), tactical hesitation (Wyatt), and miscalculation (Essex) repeatedly prove decisive even when numbers favour the rebels.

Government Response

StartSimnel Rebellion— 1487
Henry consulted the Great Council, paraded the real Warwick at St Paul's, and raised ~15,000 royal troops. Crushed at the Battle of Stoke — 4,000 rebel deaths; Lincoln killed.
MiddleKett's Rebellion— 1549
First a royal force under the Marquis of Northampton was defeated. Warwick then deployed 12,000 troops and crushed the rebels at Dussindale — ~3,000 killed; Kett hanged.
EndIrishTyrone's Rebellion— 1593–1603
17,000 troops under Essex failed; Mountjoy then deployed with 15,000 troops; 30,000 English troops in Ireland by 1603. Cost ~£2 million — the largest military commitment of any Tudor rebellion.
Synthesis Government response grows in scale and sophistication — from one decisive battle at Stoke under Henry VII, to standing armies of 30,000+ in Elizabeth's Ireland and a ~£2m bill for a single rebellion.
BP3.

Impact on / Threat to Government

Monarchy

StartPerkin Warbeck's Rebellion— 1491–99
Warbeck posed as Richard, Duke of York, backed by 3 foreign powers (France, Burgundy, Scotland). Henry issued 51 attainders against English supporters, including Sir William Stanley (Henry's step-uncle and Lord Chamberlain). Lasted 8 years.
MiddleWyatt's Rebellion— 1554
Rebels reached Ludgate, 3 miles from the City. Norfolk's troops partly defected to Wyatt. Only Mary's Guildhall speech saved her throne.
EndIrishTyrone's Rebellion— 1593–1603
Over 6,000 rebel fighters at peak; English humiliated at Yellow Ford (1598); Spanish landed ~3,000 troops at Kinsale (1601). Cost ~£2 million — the most expensive single threat to Elizabeth's government.
Synthesis The most serious threats sit at the start (Warbeck) and end (Tyrone) of the period — both featuring sustained foreign backing and existential risk to royal authority. Domestic risings (Wyatt) came close, but only foreign-backed rebellions truly endangered Tudor monarchs.

Government Policy

StartYorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Northern counties were subsequently exempted from the tax — rebels achieved their core demand despite military collapse. 6 executed including John Chamber.
MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Statute of Uses repealed (1536); Council of the North reformed; dissolutions paused 1537–39 — a direct policy retreat after the rebellion.
EndIrishFitzmaurice Fitzgerald's Rebellions— 1569 & 1579
Led directly to the Munster Plantations — a permanent settlement of English landowners that reshaped Irish governance. 800 executed 1569–72.
Synthesis Rebellions repeatedly forced Tudor policy U-turns or accelerations — Henry VII retreated on tax, Henry VIII paused dissolutions, Elizabeth doubled down with plantation in Ireland. Successful suppression did not always mean policy stayed unchanged.

Government Officials & Nobles

StartYorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Henry Percy, Earl of Northumberland and head of the tax commission, was murdered by rebels while collecting the tax — a direct strike on a leading royal official.
MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
178 executed in retribution including Robert Aske, Lord Darcy, and ~20 clergy. Bigod's secondary revolt was used as the pretext for mass execution of officials and gentry implicated in the rising.
EndEssex Revolt— 1601
3 executed (Essex, Merrick, Cuffe); Earl of Rutland fined £30,000; the Earl of Southampton and 12 deputy lieutenants also implicated. Decisive removal of a circle of disaffected nobles.
Synthesis Tudor rebellions consistently took out a top tier of officials and nobles — from murdered tax commissioners under Henry VII, to mass clerical execution under Henry VIII, to the last nobleman executed for revolt in 1601.
BP4.

Maintenance of Political Stability

Crown

StartPerkin Warbeck's Rebellion— 1491–99
Henry removed all 3 foreign backers through diplomacy: Treaties of Étaples (France) and Ayton (Scotland) cut off all foreign support before each invasion could gain traction. Stanley pre-emptively arrested in 1495.
MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Henry used 2 truces and 2 false pardons at Doncaster to buy time, then used Bigod's secondary revolt as the pretext for mass retribution (178 executed) — stability through deception, not force.
EndIrishTyrone's Rebellion— 1593–1603
Required the entire decade to suppress. Mountjoy with 15,000 troops; 30,000 by 1603; cost ~£2 million; O'Neill paradoxically pardoned; Ulster Plantation established to lock in long-term stability.
Synthesis Crown stability rested on different tools across the period — diplomacy under Henry VII, deception under Henry VIII, and prolonged military attrition under Elizabeth. The cost of stability rose with every reign.

Nobility

StartLovel & Stafford Rebellion— 1486
No noble defections beyond the three leaders; Staffords arrested at Culham Church sanctuary; Lovel fled abroad — no battle fought. Loyal nobility starved the rebellion of momentum.
MiddlePilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Significant noble defection — Lord Darcy surrendered Pontefract Castle to the rebels and Lord Hussey (chamberlain to Princess Mary) backed the Lincolnshire Rising. Both later executed among the 178. The clearest noble-loyalty failure of the period.
EndNorthern Earls' Rebellion— 1569
Northumberland could only raise £20 between himself and Westmorland to pay 1,000 footmen — 600 deserted on the spot. The last significant noble-led revolt in English history.
Synthesis Noble loyalty held under Henry VII, fractured dramatically in 1536 (with Darcy and Hussey defecting), but by 1569 the great northern magnates couldn't even pay their own retainers — the noble rebellion was effectively dead.

Local Authority

StartCornish Rebellion— 1497
Local authority failed entirely — up to 15,000 rebels marched from Cornwall and got as far as Blackheath unchecked; Henry had to recall troops from Scotland to engage them. 1,000 rebel deaths at Blackheath; Somerset and Cornwall fined £14,000 collectively.
MiddleKett's Rebellion— 1549
Local response failed initially — the Marquis of Northampton was defeated; Norwich was occupied for 7 weeks. Required Warwick + 12,000 troops from the centre to crush the rising at Dussindale.
EndOxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Sheriffs were pre-alerted by informants; 4 conspirators arrested before the rising could start; leaders Bradshaw and Burton tortured and executed. Local intelligence shut the plot down at source.
Synthesis This is the clearest trajectory of the period: local authority was almost helpless against the great mass risings of Henry VII (Cornish reached Blackheath) and Edward VI (Kett occupied Norwich), but by the 1590s local intelligence and pre-emption shut plots down before they began.
