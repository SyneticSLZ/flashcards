BP1.

Causes of Rebellions

Religious

Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
The largest Tudor rebellion — up to 50,000 rebels across 7 northern counties under the Five Wounds banner, led by Robert Aske. Presented 24 Pontefract Articles covering the dissolution of 100+ monasteries, restoration of Princess Mary, and the 1534 Subsidy Act.
Western (Prayer Book) Rebellion— 1549
~7,000 Devon and Cornwall rebels besieged Exeter for 6 weeks, calling the new English Prayer Book a "Christmas game." Presented 14 articles; led by tailor Thomas Underhill and the vicar Robert Welsh.
Northern Earls' Rebellion— 1569
Earls of Northumberland (converted Catholic, 1567) and Westmorland led ~6,000 rebels across 4 counties hoping to release Mary Queen of Scots. Notably 90% of rebels were NOT tenants of the rebel earls — broad independent religious motivation.

Socio-Economic

Yorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Protest against a £100,000 parliamentary grant for the French war — northern counties felt traditionally exempt as they bore Scottish border costs. Made worse by a bad harvest in 1488. Tax commissioner Henry Percy was murdered while collecting the tax.
Kett's Rebellion— 1549
16,000 Norfolk rebels camped on Mousehold Heath for 7 weeks. Issued 29 demands — 17 targeting enclosures and rents (rents up 30% since 1548; wheat prices up 50% in 1548). 60% of Norfolk land held by just 46 gentry families — historians describe it as the closest thing to a class war of any Tudor rebellion.
Amicable Grant Crisis— 1525
Wolsey demanded a non-parliamentary forced loan of up to 33% on clergy and 16.5% on laity for war with France, on top of £260,000 in unrepaid forced loans from 1522. Came amid rising unemployment and falling wool prices. Raised £0.

Political

Simnel Rebellion— 1487
Lambert Simnel posed as the Earl of Warwick, backed by the Earl of Lincoln (Richard III's named heir) and ~2,000 Burgundian mercenaries funded by Margaret of Burgundy. The Earl of Kildare and ~40 Irish nobles also backed the rising — a foreign-backed dynastic challenge to Henry VII's throne.
Wyatt's Rebellion— 1554
~3,000 Kent rebels marched on London opposing Mary I's marriage to Philip of Spain. Wyatt told a colleague "you may not so much as name religion" — he framed it as national defence, not religion. Lasted just 18 days; rebels reached Ludgate, 3 miles from the City.
Essex Revolt— 1601
The Earl of Essex — suspended from the Privy Council and ruined after losing his sweet wine patent — marched ~300 supporters through London. Lasted just 12 hours. The last noble rebellion in English history.
BP2.

Frequency & Nature

Lack of Support

Oxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Bartholomew Steer planned to assemble at Enslow Hill — only 4 men turned up. Illustrates the sharp decline in popular appetite for rebellion by the end of the Tudor period.
Essex Revolt— 1601
Only ~300 supporters; Essex expected the mayor and sheriff of London to back him — they did not. The City refused to rise; he reached only Fenchurch Street before retreating by river.
Perkin Warbeck's Rebellion— 1491–99
Final invasion attracted only 1,500 Cornish miners, artisans and farmers — not one was a noble or gentleman.

Poor Leadership

Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Robert Aske had 40,000 men at Pontefract but "had no wish to do battle" with Norfolk's troops — he wrote ordering rebels not to fight. Henry's 2 false pardons were never overcome.
Wyatt's Rebellion— 1554
Wyatt was a good strategist but poor tactician — he understood the need for nation-wide support but delayed entry into London when time was of the essence. Co-conspirators in Leicestershire, Devon and Herefordshire all failed to rise.
Kett's Rebellion— 1549
Rebels fatally abandoned their strong Mousehold hilltop position to fight in open ground at Dussindale against 12,000 trained royal troops — ~3,000 killed.

Government Response

Simnel Rebellion— 1487
Henry consulted the Great Council, paraded the real Warwick at St Paul's, and raised ~15,000 royal troops. Crushed at the Battle of Stoke — 4,000 rebel deaths; Lincoln killed.
Cornish Rebellion— 1497
Henry recalled troops from Scotland; 1,000 rebel deaths at Blackheath; all 3 leaders (Audley, Flamank, Joseph) executed; Somerset and Cornwall fined £14,000 collectively.
Northern Earls' Rebellion— 1569
Walsingham's intelligence tracked the plot; Mary moved to Coventry; 12,000 royal troops assembled — rebels fled without battle; 450–600 executed; raids burned ~300 Scottish villages.
BP3.

Impact on / Threat to Government

Monarchy

Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Henry VIII could not raise comparable forces — described as the most serious domestic threat of his reign. Required 2 truces at Doncaster simply to buy time, with Aske commanding 40,000 at Pontefract.
Wyatt's Rebellion— 1554
Rebels reached Ludgate, 3 miles from the City. Norfolk's troops partly defected to Wyatt. Only Mary's Guildhall speech saved her throne.
Northumberland's Rebellion— 1553
Northumberland married his son Guildford to Lady Jane Grey and manipulated Edward VI's Devise to exclude Mary and Elizabeth. Lady Jane reigned just 9 days before Mary's claim won out.

Government Policy

Yorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Northern counties were subsequently exempted from the tax — rebels achieved their core demand despite military collapse. 6 executed including John Chamber.
Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Statute of Uses repealed (1536); Council of the North reformed; dissolutions paused 1537–39. A direct policy retreat after the rebellion.
Oxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Despite mustering only 4 men, the rising prompted the Tillage Act 1597 and the Husbandry Act as direct policy responses to enclosure unrest.

Government Officials & Nobles

Yorkshire Rebellion— 1489
Henry Percy, Earl of Northumberland and head of the tax commission, was murdered by rebels while collecting the tax — a direct strike on a leading royal official.
Amicable Grant Crisis— 1525
Henry VIII used the "neat let out" of blaming Wolsey — the government "emerged with credit and the king enhanced his undeserved reputation for generosity." Wolsey's authority never recovered.
Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
178 executed in retribution including Robert Aske, Lord Darcy, and ~20 clergy. Bigod's secondary revolt was used as the pretext for mass execution.
Essex Revolt— 1601
3 executed (Essex, Merrick, Cuffe); Earl of Rutland fined £30,000. Decisive removal of a circle of disaffected nobles.
BP4.

Maintenance of Political Stability

Crown

Simnel Rebellion— 1487
Henry consulted the Great Council, paraded the real Warwick at St Paul's, and raised ~15,000 royal troops. The presence of 2,000 German mercenaries on the rebel side actually deterred English from joining as the army moved south. Decisive Crown action ended the rising at Stoke.
Perkin Warbeck's Rebellion— 1491–99
Henry removed all 3 foreign backers through diplomacy: Treaties of Étaples (France) and Ayton (Scotland) cut off all foreign support before each invasion could gain traction. Stanley pre-emptively arrested in 1495.
Pilgrimage of Grace— 1536
Henry used 2 truces and 2 false pardons at Doncaster to buy time, then used Bigod's secondary revolt as the pretext for mass retribution (178 executed).

Nobility

Lovel & Stafford Rebellion— 1486
No noble defections beyond the three leaders; Staffords arrested at Culham Church sanctuary; Lovel fled — no battle fought. Loyal nobility starved the rebellion of momentum.
Cornish Rebellion— 1497
Despite up to 15,000 marchers, the rising failed without meaningful noble support — only one impoverished peer (Lord Audley) joined. 44 priests and 22 gentry enlisted, but the absence of senior magnates was decisive.
Northern Earls' Rebellion— 1569
Northumberland could only raise £20 between himself and Westmorland to pay 1,000 footmen — 600 deserted on the spot. The last significant noble-led revolt in English history.

Local Authority

Oxfordshire Rebellion— 1596
Sheriffs were pre-alerted by informants; 4 conspirators were arrested before the rising could even start; leaders Bradshaw and Burton tortured and executed. Local intelligence shut the plot down at source.
Essex Revolt— 1601
City gates closed; the mayor and sheriff of London refused to back Essex; intelligence pre-empted every move. Essex surrendered in 12 hours.
Kett's Rebellion— 1549
Local response failed initially — the Marquis of Northampton was defeated — before Warwick deployed 12,000 troops and crushed the rising at Dussindale (~3,000 killed). Demonstrates that local authority alone could not always contain the largest risings.
